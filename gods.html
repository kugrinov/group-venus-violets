The 12 Greek Olympians

Aphrodite

Aphrodite is known to be the goddess of love and beauty. She played significant roles in commerce, war, and politics, and as one of the progenitors of the Trojan War. She was worshipped as a goddess of the sea and a protector towards the sailors. Common symbols associated with Aphrodite include doves, apples, scallop shells, and mirrors.

Athena

Athena is the goddess of wisdom, war, and craft. She is known as the patroness of Athens and is usually depicted as a warrior, armed with a spear, shield, and helmet. Athena was considered a master strategist for war that was balanced with a creative side stemming from weaving baskets and crafting tools or weapons. Her symbols include the owl and the olive tree.

Apollo

Apollo is the god of music, light, and healing. He's also associated with archery, poetry, and the arts. In addition to these roles, Apollo was seen as a protector of herds and a figure who could bring both healing and plague. He is typically associated with the sun due to his physical characteristics along with his spirit that is often very bright and warm. His symbols include the sun and the lyre.

Artemis

Artemis is the goddess of the hunt, the moon, archery, and the wilderness. She is Apollo’s twin, and the older twin of the two. Artemis is often associated with wild animals, childbirth, and the protection of young girls. Her symbols include the bow and arrow and often the moon or scared animals like the deer.

Demeter

Demeter is the goddess of the harvest, agriculture, and grain. She kept the earth thriving with food, vegetation, and nature. Her most popular myth derives from her daughter Persephone who was abducted by Hades and led to the changing of the seasons. Her symbols include wheat ears, the winged serpent, and the cornucopia.

Dionysus

Dionysus is the god of wine, pleasure, and festivity. He was also associated with ecstasy and the theatre, where his festivals were a significant part of the cultural landscape. He is known for being very dramatic, drunk, and a god that loved to party. His symbols included vines, ivy, a thyrsus (a staff tipped with a pinecone), and a goblet of wine.

Hades

Hades is the god of the underworld and ruler of the dead. Hades took rule over the shadowy depths of the underworld while his brothers Zeus and Poiseidon took over ruling the seas and skies after defeating the titans. One of his most famous myths involves his abduction of Persephone and the pomegranate seeds he used to keep her there for 6 months. He also is usually shown with Cerberus, the three headed dog that acts as a guardian in the underworld. His symbols include cornucopia, scepters, and keys.

Hephaestus

Hephaestus is the god of blacksmithing and metal work. He is often seen as a figure of strength and vulnerability in mythology and is known for his superior craftsmanship of weapons, armor, and tools, as well as many mythical or magical objects for gods and mortals. His most notable symbols include the anvil, the hammer, and sometimes volcanoes.

Hermes

Hermes is the messenger god. He is best known for his speed, cunningness, and ability to navigate both mythical and mortal worlds with ease. He is a patron god of merchants, thieves, and travelers. He often delivered messages and prophecies between gods and men, delivering items that could change people’s paths significantly. His symbols often include the winged helmet and the staff.

Hera

Hera is the goddess of women, marriage, and childbirth. She is the wife of Zeus and considered the queen of the gods. She was a respectable queen, but was also known to be vengeful, especially due to her husband’s horrible habit of committing adultery. Her symbols include peacocks and crowns.

Poseidon

Poseidon is the god of the sea and storms. He was known to have a horrible temper which is most notable in his myth where he battles against Odysseus time and again. He is also believed to be the patron god of horses and the creator of horses. His most notable symbols include tridedents, horses, and dolphins.

Zeus

Zeus is the god of the sky, thunder, and lighting. He is known as the king of the gods. Even over his brothers Poesidon and Hades. He is often referred to as the "father" of both gods and men, representing order and justice. He is well known for his affairs and the many children born from those affairs afterward. The most common symbols to represent Zeus are the lightning bolt, the eagle, and sometimes the bull.
